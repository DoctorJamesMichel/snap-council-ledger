# SCL-3.1 — Multi-Agent Orchestration Infrastructure (MAOI)
## Structural Evaluation Template v0.1

**Status:** Foundational Baseline  
**Scope:** Early-Phase AI Orchestration Systems  
**Authority:** Advisory Only  
**Intent:** Sustainability Mapping, Not Verdict  
**Ledger Class:** Ecosystem Evaluation (SCL-3)

---

## I. Entity Classification

A system qualifies as MAOI if it:

- Orchestrates multiple AI models or agents.
- Chains research, reasoning, coding, design, or deployment tasks.
- Operates primarily as execution infrastructure rather than sovereign deliberative council.
- Externalizes final responsibility to human operator or enterprise.

**Clarification:**  
MAOI is not a council class.  
It is execution-oriented coordination infrastructure.

---

## II. Governance Metadata Visibility

Evaluate presence or absence of:

- Dissent trace export
- Minority reasoning preservation
- Uncertainty encoding
- Assumption declaration
- Decision lineage logging

**Early-Phase Expectation:**  
Low to minimal visibility.

**Sustainability Quotient (SQ) Risk Vector:**  
Opaque reasoning reduces traceability and increases correlated blind spot probability.

---

## III. Responsibility Topology Mapping

Identify:

- Who authorizes execution?
- Who owns downstream consequence?
- Is orchestration bounded or autonomous?
- Is human override structurally preserved?

**SQ Sensitivity:**  
If execution velocity increases while responsibility clarity remains informal or externalized, long-term sustainability pressure increases.

---

## IV. Correlation Density Assessment

Estimate:

- Foundation model overlap likelihood.
- Shared training substrate probability.
- Shared safety tuning bias.
- Orchestration convergence amplification effects.

**Current Ecosystem Assumption:**  
High baseline substrate correlation across frontier models.

**SQ Risk Vector:**  
Plurality illusion without substrate diversity increases systemic fragility.

---

## V. Velocity–Governance Alignment

Map relationship between:

- Task execution acceleration
- Embedded friction mechanisms
- Built-in perturbation equivalents
- Auditability features
- Post-execution review loops

**Key Question:**  
Does governance scale proportionally with execution velocity?

---

## VI. Perturbation Compatibility

Evaluate whether MAOI architecture:

- Allows external perturbation injection
- Exports sufficient metadata for PL-style review
- Preserves non-consensus reasoning artifacts
- Supports consequence review cycles

If not, SNAP engagement remains observational.

---

## VII. Provisional Decay Classification

Classify domain as:

- Rapid (Quarter-scale evolution)
- Moderate (Annual-scale evolution)
- Slow (Multi-year)

**Initial MAOI Classification:**  
Rapid to Moderate

**Implication:**  
Short review interval required until structural stabilization observed.

---

## VIII. Preliminary Sustainability Posture Categories

After evaluation, categorize system as:

1. Velocity-Dominant Emerging Infrastructure
2. Balanced Transitional Infrastructure
3. Governance-Embedded Infrastructure
4. Opaque High-Correlation Infrastructure

Categories may evolve over time.

---

## IX. Advisory Output Constraints

SNAP shall:

- Record structural observations.
- Avoid endorsement language.
- Avoid certification framing.
- Preserve advisory-only posture.
- Schedule adaptive review based on decay rate.

---

## X. Review Marker Requirement

Every MAOI assessment must include:

- Date of baseline recording
- Trigger conditions for next review
- Perturbation Layer (PL) activation record (if applicable)

Time remains arbiter.
